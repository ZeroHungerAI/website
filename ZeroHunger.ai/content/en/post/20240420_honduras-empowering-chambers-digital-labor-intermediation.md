In April 2024, our team traveled to Tegucigalpa, Honduras to work on one of our most ambitious projects: helping an entire national network of chambers of commerce modernize how they connect workers with employers. The project, funded through Germany's BMZ development cooperation via sequa and bbw, aims to transform how vocational training and labor placement services work across Honduras — not through a top-down government program, but through the country's own business associations.

This is the story of what we found, what we built, and what it means for tens of thousands of workers and businesses.

## The Challenge: A Fragmented Labor Market in a Growing Economy

Honduras has a paradox familiar to many developing economies: businesses struggle to find qualified workers while unemployment, especially among young people, remains stubbornly high. The disconnect is not just about skills — it is about infrastructure. There is no single, trusted system that connects job seekers with employers at a national scale.

The country's network of chambers of commerce — 43 chambers organized under the national federation FEDECAMARA, spanning all 17 departments — is uniquely positioned to solve this. Chambers already have relationships with roughly 20,000 member enterprises. They understand regional labor markets. What they lacked was the digital infrastructure and operational capacity to turn that knowledge into a scalable job matching service.

One chamber had already proven the concept. The Cámara de Comercio e Industrias de Tegucigalpa (CCIT) had built and operated **Promuévete**, a labor intermediation platform that in 2023 alone processed 3,442 job vacancies, sent 27,101 CVs to employers, and placed 298 people in jobs — achieving a 92% client satisfaction rate. The question was: could this success be replicated across 42 more chambers, many of them smaller, less resourced, and in regions with very different labor dynamics?

## Our Role: IT Consulting for a National Platform

ZeroHunger.ai was brought in as IT expert to support the digital pillar of this transformation. Our engagement ran from February through April 2024, covering three phases:

**Phase 1 — Remote Analysis (February)**
We conducted a comprehensive baseline assessment of the existing platforms, including security audits, performance analysis, data protection compliance review, and UX evaluation. This gave us a clear picture of what worked, what did not, and what would need to change for a national rollout.

**Phase 2 — Solution Design (March)**
Through weekly remote sessions with chamber stakeholders across Honduras, we presented improvement recommendations, discussed architecture options for the national platform (**Emplea Más**), and aligned on a technical roadmap. Critical decisions had to be made: one national platform versus regional instances, centralized versus distributed technical support, and how to handle the significant variation in digital maturity across chambers.

**Phase 3 — On-Site Workshop Week (April 15–19, Tegucigalpa)**
The core of the engagement was an intensive week of workshops at CCIT and FEDECAMARA headquarters in Tegucigalpa. Over five days, we worked directly with chamber staff, the development team, and federation leadership on:

- IT security analysis and hardening recommendations
- Platform performance optimization
- Data protection strategy aligned with Honduran regulations
- SEO and digital visibility improvements
- UX redesign for both employers and job seekers
- Integration architecture for connecting chambers, training providers, and the national platform
- Operational processes for sustainable platform management

The workshop was not just about technology. A significant portion of the week was spent on organizational questions — how chambers should structure their new labor intermediation departments, how to train staff who had never managed a digital platform, and how to build a governance model that would keep the system running long after the project funding ended.

## The Platform: From Promuévete to Emplea Más

The technical heart of the project is the evolution from CCIT's proven **Promuévete** platform into **Emplea Más**, a national system operated by FEDECAMARA that all 43 member chambers can use.

The platform connects three user groups:

**Job seekers** can create profiles, browse vacancies filtered by region and sector, track their applications, and access training courses — all through a web interface optimized for mobile devices. For those without reliable internet access, chamber staff can register candidates manually, ensuring the system does not exclude the people who need it most.

**Employers** can post vacancies, review applications, access candidate evaluations, and communicate directly with applicants through an integrated chat system. WhatsApp notifications keep both sides informed without requiring constant platform access — a critical feature in a market where many workers and small business owners are mobile-first.

**Chamber administrators** manage their regional labor market: validating candidate profiles, running recruitment processes, tracking placement metrics, and reporting outcomes to the federation and project funders.

The architecture — built on .NET Core with a Quasar frontend and integrated Moodle for training — was designed to be maintained by a small central team rather than requiring technical staff at every chamber.

## Lessons from the Ground

Working in Tegucigalpa reinforced several lessons we have seen across international development projects that involve technology:

**Technology is the easy part.** The platform existed and worked. The real challenge was organizational: getting 43 independent chambers, each with their own culture, capacity, and political dynamics, to adopt a shared digital infrastructure and new service processes. The strategic relationship between FEDECAMARA as the national federation and CCIT as the largest and most advanced chamber required careful navigation.

**Design for the least connected user.** In Honduras, where smartphone and data costs remain significant barriers for many workers and micro-enterprises, we could not assume universal digital access. The manual registration pathway, WhatsApp notifications, and mobile-optimized interface were not nice-to-haves — they were essential to the platform's mission of inclusive labor intermediation.

**Sustainability requires a business model.** The project is funded through international development cooperation, but the platform needs to survive beyond the funding cycle. We worked with FEDECAMARA to design a service model where chambers generate revenue from HR services — recruitment process management, candidate evaluation, and training delivery — rather than relying solely on membership fees. This transforms chambers from passive membership organizations into active service providers.

**Local ownership is non-negotiable.** The development team building the platform — a young Honduran company called GrupoAT — was already embedded in the chamber ecosystem. Our role was to advise, audit, and accelerate, not to build the system ourselves. The technical knowledge needed to stay in Honduras.

## What Comes Next

Our engagement delivered a comprehensive technical roadmap, security recommendations, and operational guidelines. The chambers are now working to:

- Roll out Emplea Más to additional chambers beyond CCIT
- Train chamber staff in platform administration and service delivery
- Establish partnerships with INFOP, the national vocational training institute, for accredited training programs
- Build the monitoring and reporting infrastructure needed to demonstrate impact to funders and government stakeholders

The Honduras BBW project represents exactly the kind of work ZeroHunger.ai was created to do: using technology expertise to amplify the impact of organizations already working to reduce poverty and improve livelihoods. The chambers of commerce are not aid recipients — they are business institutions serving their communities. Our job was to help them do it better, faster, and at a scale that matches the challenge.
